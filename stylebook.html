Fourth Edition, Produced August 2023

If no entry exists for a question encountered during production, please keep note of the issue and report the matter to a copy editor.

A


abbreviations and acronyms |

It is often tempting to pepper newspaper writing with acronyms or abbreviations familiar to the writer. Use caution. They are often unfamiliar to the reader. Yale, with its compendium of abbreviations and acronyms for everything from parking lots to libraries, provides many such temptations. This booklet and the AP Stylebook include individual entries for many particular abbreviations and acronyms. General principles:

  • BEFORE A NAME Always abbreviate the following titles when used before a full name outside direct quotations: Dr., Gov., Lt. Gov., Rep., the Rev., and Sen. See the AP Stylebook for military titles. Spell out all except Dr., Mr. and Mrs. when used before a name in direct quotation. See courtesy titles and legislative titles in this site for more information. See the AP Stylebook for guidelines on religious titles. See titles in this booklet for general titles, organizational titles, job titles and professional titles. See also professor. Only medical doctors receive the title of Dr., not Ph.D. holders.
  • AFTER A NAME Abbreviate junior and senior after an individual’s name. Do not use an intervening comma: Michael Jones III, John DeStefano Jr. Abbreviate company, corporation, incorporated and limited when used after the name of a corporate entity: AOL Time Warner Inc., Philip Morris Cos. Inc. See also individual entries in the AP Stylebook for major corporations.

Do not create acronyms that do not exist. For acronyms of organization names that are used by some people, especially organization members, but may not be familiar to many readers, spell out the organization name on all references or introduce the acronym if the name is excessively bulky: The Movimiento Estudiantil Chicano de Aztlan, or MEChA, is Yale’s largest student organization for students of Mexican descent. Do not use parentheses to introduce acronyms. See individual entries for guidance on many organizations. See also the Index of Yale Buildings at the end of this booklet. For acronyms that end in “s,” like Yale-NUS, use only an apostrophe to create a possessive. Do not add an additional “s.”

abortion |

In most cases, it is acceptable to refer to abortion-rights and anti-abortion activist groups with the labels they prefer. Pro-life and pro-choice are acceptable in reference to the standard two sides of the debate.

If, however, it is particularly important to maintain a more pronounced appearance of impartiality, use abortion-rights group or anti-abortion group or something similar.

academic degrees |

For degrees that consist of two capital letters, write out the name of the degree. Do not use B.A., B.S., M.A., M.S., Ph.D., etc. except in a direct quotation (MD-PhD is acceptable): Joe Kagan, who received a doctorate in history from Yale, teaches at Ohio State University. Susan Kagan, who graduated from Yale with a bachelor’s degree in chemistry, went to graduate school in England. Yale gave an honorary master’s degree to each new member of the Yale Corporation who did not have a postgraduate degree.

For degrees that consist of three capital letters, abbreviations without periods are acceptable if writing out the name of the degree is awkward: King, who has an MBA from Cornell University, will serve as the next vice president for finance and administration. Other common three-capital degrees include master’s of public health (MPH) and master’s of fine arts (MFA). See joint-degree programs.

See also class year and master of arts, master of science.

accents and diacritical marks |

Go ahead; use them in people’s names or official titles. Do not use accents on words in their generic form. E.g., Dean of Student Affairs Camille Lizzaríbar.

accuse |

A person is accused of, not with, a crime. See also allege, arrest and indict.

A.D. |

Acceptable in all references for anno Domini: in the year of the Lord. Should be used for all years that may be unclear to many readers. The abbreviation goes before the year: The Nicene Council met in A.D. 325 to adopt the Nicene Creed. Do not write: The fourth century A.D. It is sufficient to write the fourth century. Do not use the abbreviation C.E. See B.C.

addresses |

Use the abbreviations Ave., Blvd. and St. only with a numbered address: 202 York St. Spell out and capitalize them when part of a formal street name without a number: York Street. Lowercase when used alone or with more than one street name: Elm and Howe streets.

Broadway in Downtown, New Haven is just Broadway, not Broadway St. or Ave.

Always spell out alley, drive, road etc., and capitalize when part of a formal street name. Lowercase when used alone or with more than one street name.

Always use figures for an address, unless the address starts a sentence: The Provost’s Office moved to 1 Hillhouse Ave. But: One Hillhouse Ave. was the home of Undergraduate Career Services for 10 years.

For addresses in New Haven, list only the street address. For addresses outside of New Haven, list the city and, if outside Connecticut, the state as well.

When identifying individuals, it is not necessary to list their exact home address. Usually, writing their street name, for New Haven residents, or their town, for non-New Haven residents, is sufficient. Roger Thompson of Munson Street and Sue Smith of Hamden.

See the AP Stylebook for more details.

administration |

Do not capitalize. Without modification, it is assumed to refer collectively to Yale’s administration: Sources in the administration said yesterday that tuition would rise by 2.9 percent.

administrators |

Acceptable for officers and higher-level, nonfaculty employees of Yale University. Use a more specific word if possible.

admissions office |

adopted |

Like race, adoptive status should only be mentioned in a story if it is relevant.

adviser |

Not advisor. The following are never capitalized: academic adviser, sophomore adviser, junior adviser etc.

affect, effect |

Affect, as a verb, means to influence: The game will affect the standings. Less frequently, it can mean to make a pretense of having: He affected a European accent. As a noun, it is best avoided. It is occasionally used in psychology to describe an emotion, but there is no need for it in everyday language.

Effect, as a verb, means to cause: He will effect many changes in the company. As a noun, effect means result: He miscalculated the effect of his actions. The effect was overwhelming.

Afghan, afghani |

Afghan is an adjective; afghani is the unit of currency in Afghanistan. That is, an Afghan, a person from Afghanistan, pays for his purchases in Kabul with afghanis.

African American |

The preferred term is Black. Use African American only in quotations or the names of organizations or if individuals describe themselves so. AP Style has changed to no hyphen, and see also departments and programs for the use of African American Studies, the academic department at Yale and the associated major. Note the capitalization of Black.

Afro-American Cultural Center at Yale |

afterward |

Not afterwards.

ages |

Always use figures, never words: Susan Jones, 5, attends first grade at Hometown Elementary. Frank’s cousin is 3 years old. The woman is in her 30s. Hyphenate when ages are adjectival or when they substitute for a noun: A 5-year-old boy. The race is for 3-year-olds.

alder |

Alders, of whom there are 30 on New Haven’s Board of Alders, are elected to two-year terms and are not subject to term limits. Alder is capitalized when used as formal titles before a full name. It is lowercase in all other uses. Do not use alderman, alderwoman, alderpersons or alderpeople. It is not necessary to state the ward an alder represents if it is not relevant to the story. If the ward is provided, state what area of the city the alder represents instead of using the ward number, as follows: Hill Alder Dolores Colón ’91. See also Board of Alders and ward.

See https://newhaven-ct.legistar.com/Departments.aspx.

Aldermanic Chambers |

Can be used interchangeably with Board of Alders Chamber. Located at 165 Church St., second floor.

allege |

Use with care and always specify the source of the allegation. Do not use it as a routine qualifier when there is no person doing the alleging, although it can be used repeatedly in crime-related stories when it is clear that police or prosecutors are the source of the allegations. Otherwise, use words such as apparent, ostensible or reputed. See the AP Stylebook for details on how to handle criminal allegations. Do not use terms with criminal connotations to refer to something not criminal in nature. See also accuse, arrest and indict.

al-Qaida |

The AP preferred spelling of the terrorist organization formerly headed by Osama bin Laden. Note that al is an article (akin to the) and need not be included in headlines.

All-Ivy |

A sports term used to bestow distinction on a varsity player within the Ivy League. Used in conjunction with first, second team. He was named first-team All-Ivy. She was named to the All-Ivy First Team. He was given First Team honors. She was given All-Ivy First Team honors. He is an All-Ivy player. She is a first-team All-Ivy player.

alumnus |

Without other identification, assumed to be a graduate of Yale College. Alumnus refers to a man. The plural is alumni. Alumna refers to a woman. The plural is alumnae. Use alumni for a group of mixed gender. Do not use alum or alums except in headlines. For identifying Yale graduates with a year of graduation, see class year. See also academic degrees.

AM |

Acceptable in all references to the amplitude modulation system of radio transmission. For guidelines on names and frequencies of radio stations, see radio stations.

a.m. |

Lowercase, with periods, for times. Avoid the redundant 10 a.m. this morning.

amid |

Not amidst.

among, between |

In general, use between with two items, but among with more than two. But when comparing two groups of items, between is the correct word: Negotiations on a debate format are under way between the network and the Bush and Kerry campaigns. See the AP Stylebook for details. Do not use amongst for among.

ampersand |

Avoid use of ampersands except when part of a company’s formal name or the composition title. Do not use for libraries, organizations or offices on campus. Do not use in headlines.

Amtrak |

This acronym, drawn from the words American travel track by track, may be used in all references to the National Railroad Passenger Corp. Do not use AMTRAK.

See the AP Stylebook entry for more information.

Ancient Eight |

See Ivy League.

annual |

An event that is being held for the first time should not be referred to as annual.

anonymity |

When identifying sources who are not named, be sure to report accurately the terms of the agreement of anonymity. Use spoke on condition of anonymity only if the person agreed to speak on condition that his or her name not be used. If the source asks that the reporter not use the source’s last name, write Jimmy, who asked that his last name not be used, or something similar. For Yale Daily News policy on withholding names, see naming individuals.

anonymous sources |

On the one hand, the use of anonymous sources weakens a story since it casts doubt on the validity of the story in the mind of the reader. On the other hand, it may not be possible to gather and report information without promising a source protection. Some guidelines:

  • Balance the doubt caused against the information gained. Do not grant anonymity to a source who provides information that is easily obtainable elsewhere, of a generic nature or of tangential relevance.
  • Grant anonymity only if you and the source specifically discuss it. Do not turn an on-the-record conversation into a not-for-attribution conversation after the fact.
  • Print the information provided by an anonymous source only if you are fully confident in the source’s reliability.

Board of Alders |

There are 30 members of New Haven’s Board of Alders, the city government’s legislative branch, representing 30 wards in the city. See alder and ward for more detail. Use Board of Alders without a city name when referring to New Haven’s Board of Alders unless it could be confused with another town’s board. Do not abbreviate as BoA or BOA.

Retain the capitals when Board of Alders is used in full. Lowercase board when not using the full title: The Board of Alders passed a resolution last night supporting a ban on handguns in the city of New Haven, even though several city officials said the board lacks the authority to do so.

Board of Fire Commissioners |

The supervising board of the New Haven Fire Department. It comprises five fire commissioners appointed by the mayor, who also sits on the board ex officio. Avoid referring to this body as the fire commission, although it sometimes may be called that in other sources. Use the board, not BFC, on second reference.

Board of Police Commissioners |

The supervising board of the New Haven Police Department. The board comprises six police commissioners, who are appointed by the mayor for staggered three-year terms. One is appointed to serve as chairman, and the mayor also sits on the board ex officio. The board theoretically shares responsibility for policymaking with the chief of police. In practice, however, it generally restricts itself to reviewing decisions made at a lower level and overseeing long-term initiatives.

Avoid referring to this body as the police commission, although it may be sometimes called that in other sources. Use the board, not BPC, on second reference.

buildings |

bylines and taglines |

Most stories not written by the Associated Press carry a byline or a tagline. A byline is styled as follows: By John Smith. A tagline is styled as follows: —John Smith. It is right-justified beneath the story text, with a long (or em-) dash. Guidelines for bylines and taglines:

  • Briefs written by one or more reporters carry those names in the tagline in alphabetical order by last name, regardless of status: —John Jones and Mary Smith.
  • Briefs provided by the Associated Press carry the tagline —Associated Press. If the AP is the second author in a tagline, use The Associated Press: —John Jones and The Associated Press.
  • Briefs written by an editor carry the tagline —Yale Daily News.
  • Stories written by a single reporter carry that reporter’s byline.
  • Stories written or reported by more than one reporter in which all contributors participated roughly equally carry all names as bylines, styled as follows: By John Jones and Mary Smith or By John Jones, Mary Smith and Jimmy McBruce. Names are listed alphabetically in multiple-bylined stories, except that staff reporters are listed before contributing reporters.
  • Stories written or reported by more than one reporter in which one or more reporters contributed substantially less than the others carry the primary reporter’s name (or names) in the byline and the secondary reporters in the tagline. In this case, tagline style is as follows: —Staff Reporter John Public contributed to this story. Or, for contributing reporters: —Additional reporting by John Smith. If both a staff and contributing reporter need to be mentioned, use the appropriate sentences for each, conjoined by a semicolon: —Staff Reporter John Public contributed to this story; additional reporting by John Smith.

Note that a period should be included in the tagline only when it forms a complete sentence.

See also photo credit and status line.

chair |

Yale insists on using chair as the formal title of the heads of academic programs and departments and committees. Use chair when referring directly to the Yale position, but in this instance do not capitalize before a name: Yale President Richard Levin said yesterday he would name Hazel Carby chair of the new Department of African American Studies. Levin said Math Department chair Peter Jones would lead the committee.

Chairman or chairwoman indicates the title of the position, but (like professor/coach) is not capitalized before a name, unless it is the official title: Yale Daily News Chairman Joseph Lieberman ’64. Chair indicates the same position, but only in very specific contexts as mentioned above, or in organizations whose head is specified as such, e.g. GESO Chair Aaron Greenberg GRD ’18. When in doubt, use chairman. Never use chairperson or chairpeople unless preferred by the individual or organization; use chairman or chairmen when gender is unspecified or unknown, or for a group of mixed gender.

City Plan Department |

Not Planning. The department is the technical staff for the City Plan Commission and the Board of Zoning Appeals.

Civilian Review Board |

The Civilian Review Board is designed to monitor citizen complaints against New Haven police officers. It consists of one person appointed by the mayor, one by the Board of Police Commissioners, one by the Board of Alders and one from each of New Haven’s 12 community management teams. Members serve three-year staggered terms.

The Civilian Review Board operates in addition to the existing Board of Police Commissioners and the Police Department’s Internal Values and Ethics Unit. It does not hold any power of subpoena. It reviews all civilian complaints against officers. It can ask the Internal Values and Ethics Unit to investigate any complaint and may review any investigation by the unit. The board can make recommendations regarding discipline to the chief and the Board of Police Commissioners, but the commissioners and the chief are the only entities legally empowered to discipline officers.

Do not abbreviate on second reference; use the board.

class year |

The expected graduation year of current Yale graduate and undergraduate students should always be given: Laura McMullan ’07. Note that it is an apostrophe, not an opening single quote, and that the year is not separated from the name by a comma.

Alumni of Yale University should also generally be identified with the year of their graduation: Bill Clinton LAW ’73. But avoid such identification if the person is mentioned in the Yale Daily News with such frequency that the year is an encumbrance of little use. Do not give class years for senior officers, deans and administrators of the University, as well as New Haven officials who are mentioned with regularity. Do not give class years to World Fellows. Do give class years to former senior officers, deans and administrators of the University, as well as New Haven officials.

Do not give a class year if the person did not graduate, unless he or she died while a student: Suzanne Jovin ’99. Samuel Itty ’02.

If a person graduated from Yale more than 90 years ago, write “class of” bracketed by commas: Edward Bouchet, class of 1874. Frank Black, Yale Law School class of 1903.

Lowercase class of in all uses: Members of the class of 2023 attended Commencement on Sunday.

For current students of other universities, use first year, sophomore, junior or senior to designate their grade level: LeRoy Jenkins, a Harvard sophomore, agreed that Yale is way better.

The following are the abbreviations that should precede a year signifying a graduate or professional degree from Yale:

  • ARC Architecture
  • ART Art
  • DIV Divinity
  • DRA Drama
  • ENG Engineering & Applied Science
  • SPH Public Health
  • ENV School of the Environment (formerly known as FES)
  • GRD Graduate School
  • LAW Law
  • MED Medicine
  • MUS Music
  • NUR Nursing
  • SOM Management
  • JGA Jackson School of Global Affairs
  • Yale-NUS

courts |

There are two types of courts, federal and state, in New Haven.

FEDERAL: The federal court system has three primary levels: the Supreme Court, the Court of Appeals and the District Courts. Bankruptcy Court is also federal.

  • The U.S. Court of Appeals is divided into 13 circuits. Connecticut, along with New York and Vermont, falls within the 2nd Circuit. The 2nd Circuit Court of Appeals is located in New York City.
  • The U.S. District Court for the District of Connecticut has a courthouse at 141 Church St., across from the Green. There are also federal District Courts located in Bridgeport, Hartford and Waterbury.
  • The U.S. Bankruptcy Court for the District of Connecticut has an office in the Connecticut Financial Center at 157 Church St. There are also Bankruptcy Courts in Bridgeport and Hartford.
  • The formal names of courts — 2nd Circuit Court of Appeals, U.S. District Courts — are capitalized, even when plural. See the AP Stylebook for more information on the federal court system, including style rules.

STATE: The Connecticut state court system also has three main levels: the Connecticut Supreme Court, the Appellate Court and the Superior Courts.

  • The Connecticut Supreme Court is located in Hartford, as is the Appellate Court.
  • Superior Court is a term that applies to three sublevels: the Judicial District Courts, the Geographical Area Courts and the Juvenile Matters Courts. New Haven has one of each. The division between the Judicial Districts Courts and the Geographical Area Courts is not always clear, but in general more serious matters are handled in the Judicial District Courts.
  • The New Haven Judicial District Court is located at 235 Church St.
  • The court for Geographical Area 23 at New Haven is located at 121 Elm St., next to the New Haven Free Public Library.
  • The Juvenile Matters Court in New Haven is located at 239 Whalley Ave.

Note that all three courts may properly be referred to as Superior Court, and should be in most contexts. It is preferable to avoid explaining more complicated terms unless they directly relate to the story.

Information on other types of state courts, such as Probate Court, Community Court or the drug courts, may be found on the Connecticut state website or in judicial directories.

Faculty of Arts and Sciences |

Not the same thing as the Yale College faculty. It is possible to abbreviate FAS on second reference; the faculty is not acceptable on second reference. See also Joint Boards of Permanent Officers.

The ranks in the Faculty of Arts and Sciences are:

  • professor: A full, tenured member of the faculty.
  • associate professor: May be tenured. An associate professor with tenure will be promoted to professor in at most seven years. An associate professor on term may serve in that position for no more than six years.
  • assistant professor: A nontenured position. Assistant professors almost always have doctoral degrees and may serve in that position for no more than seven years.
  • lecturer convertible: A person accepted as an assistant professor who has not yet received a doctoral degree is given the rank of lecturer convertible until he receives the degree, which he must do within four semesters of appointment.
  • adjunct professor, adjunct associate professor, adjunct assistant professor: Adjunct appointments are normally on a part-time basis. Faculty at this rank do not vote at faculty meetings or in departmental hiring reviews. The particular designation (professor, associate, assistant) is commensurate with the individual’s distinction and accomplishments.
  • lecturer: An indefinitely renewable, nonpermanent position. Lecturers normally have doctoral degrees and must do substantial teaching. Appointments are for periods from one term to three years. Faculty members at this rank do not vote at faculty meetings or in departmental hiring sessions.
  • lector: A one-year, renewable appointment for individuals who teach foreign languages. Faculty members at this rank do not vote at faculty meetings or in departmental hiring reviews.
  • senior lector: A three-year, renewable appointment for experienced individuals who teach foreign languages. Faculty members at this rank do not vote at faculty meetings or in departmental hiring reviews.
  • Gibbs instructor: A nonladder position in the Mathematics Department equivalent to assistant professor, except that it is for a term of two years.
  • assistant instructor: A position equivalent to teaching assistant for individuals who are not Yale graduate students.

More detailed information can be found in the Faculty Handbook.

hyphenation |

When spelling questions involving hyphenation arise for words not listed in this stylebook, check the AP Stylebook first and Webster’s New World College Dictionary second.

For compound modifiers, use the ambiguity test: Could a reasonable person read two plausible meanings from a nonhyphenated phrase? Ambiguous phrases such as gay history professor have appeared in News headlines. Does the person in question teach and study gay history, or is he just a history professor who happens to be gay? A hyphen should have been used to resolve the ambiguity in favor of the former.

Use a hyphen to clarify individual modifiers and their targets within a compound modifier, but you may omit the hyphen IF the modifying phrase is clearly an inseparable unit (high school, financial aid, navy blue).

Never join an adverb ending in -ly to any other modifier: frequently asked questions. This is not a compound modifier; rather, the adverb is modifying the adjective and the adjective is modifying the noun.

legislative titles |

no contest, nolo contendere |

Equivalent terms for a plea that may be entered by a defendant in a criminal case. When a defendant enters this plea, it means that he is not admitting guilt but is stating that he will offer no defense. The person is then subject to being judged guilty and punished as if he had pleaded guilty or had been convicted. The principal difference is that the defendant retains the option of denying the same charge in another legal proceeding. In general, avoid referring to a person as guilty of a crime if he pleads no contest.

professor |

TITLE BEFORE A NAME Use professor, lowercase, before the name, of a person who is on the faculty or who teaches a class in Yale College or the Graduate School, regardless of his actual title: The complaint was filed by history professor Mary Smith. Do not use professor as a title before the name of a person who teaches a residential college seminar in Yale College unless he or she otherwise holds the title. Never capitalize professor before a name. Use the person’s full title or a restatement of the title if it appears after the name: The complaint was filed by Mary Smith, an associate professor in the Chemical Engineering Department. He gave the document to Joe Smith, who is a senior lector in the German department. Also acceptable: He gave the document to Ed Smith, who is an associate professor of statistics.

FACULTY OF ARTS AND SCIENCES Men and women who teach classes in Yale College and the Graduate School are given various titles by the University. These titles include: professor, associate professor, assistant professor, adjunct professor, lecturer, lector and senior lector. See also Faculty of Arts and Sciences, ladder faculty and tenure.

ADJUNCT PROFESSORS Visiting scholars who do not have the rank of professor; diplomats-, writers- or artists-in-residence; or members of the faculties of Yale’s professional schools who are not members of the Faculty of Arts and Sciences are often given the title adjunct professor if they teach in Yale College or in the Graduate School.

ENDOWED PROFESSORSHIPS Some professor hold endowed positions named after a benefactor. It is not necessary to give the full name of the endowed position unless it is relevant to the story.

EMERITUS A full professor — not an associate or assistant professor — is given the designation emeritus upon his or her retirement. It is never capitalized: The book was written by Mary Smith, a professor emeritus of history. Always use professor emeritus after a name. Do not confuse emeritus and former. A full professor who retires from Yale becomes a professor emeritus. Any professor who leaves Yale is a former professor or a former Yale professor, if he is a professor elsewhere. The plural is professors emeriti, but it is rarely used. Always place emeritus after professor.

Use professor to refer generally to a person or group of people who are on the faculty or who teach classes in Yale College or the Graduate School: Several history professors said yesterday that they would leave Yale next term. A professor in Yale’s Geology Department found a new volcano in Switzerland. If there is a chance that a person could be confused with a professor at another school, use Yale professor.

PROFESSIONAL SCHOOLS Yale’s professional schools have varying and different designations for member of their faculties and men and women who teach their classes. Follow these guidelines:

  • LAW In all references, law professor is appropriate: He wrote the book with law professor Henry Jones. Use Yale law professor if necessary to avoid confusion.
  • MEDICINE If no indication of specialty is needed, medical school professor is appropriate in all references and as a title before a name. Use Yale medical school professor if necessary to avoid confusion. Do not use medical professor. If an indication of specialty is needed, put the full title, followed by of and the specialty, after the name: Henry Jones, a professor of neurobiology at the medical school, wrote the book. See Public Health.
  • FORESTRY & ENVIRONMENTAL STUDIES If the person teaches forestry, forestry professor is appropriate in all references and as a title before a name. Some professors in the School of the Environment teach environmental science. In all cases, it is acceptable to use the full title after the name: Karen Jones, an assistant professor at the School of the Environment.
  • ART, ARCHITECTURE, DRAMA AND MUSIC Always use the full title and identify the school after the name to avoid confusion with professors who teach these subjects in Yale College: John Smith, an adjunct professor at the Art School, designed the facade. Mary Jones, a playwright-in-residence at the David Geffen School of Drama, wrote the play. Bob Martinez, an adjunct professor of composition at the Music School, led the band.

that, which |

That can usually be omitted when following said and similar words. Include that when the words after said can be mistaken for its object: The mayor announced that his party's tax program would undergo an official investigation. Also include that to avoid ambiguity when there is a time element or a subordinate clause introduced by although, since, while or something similar: The governor said today that she would sign the income-tax bill. She said that although the tax would bankrupt most citizens, the government needed the money.

In compound sentences it is sometimes necessary to use and that in the second half of the compound. In such cases, that should be used in the first half to maintain parallel structure: The mayor said that he might run for governor and that, if he did, Joan Smith would be his campaign manager.

Also be wary of the difference between that and which. The former is a restrictive pronoun: The clause it introduces must be essential to the meaning of the noun it modifies. The latter introduces nonessential information. An example should suffice to show the difference:

  • (EXAMPLE) The car that I have in my garage is for sale. In this sentence, the clause is necessary to indicate which car is being spoken about. Perhaps another car, in the driveway, is not for sale.
  • (EXAMPLE) The car, which I have in my garage, is for sale. The clause only provides more information about the car; it is not necessary to the car’s identification.

Note the use of commas in the examples. Clauses introduced by that are never set off by commas. Clauses introduced by which almost always are.

Yale Political Union |

Both the YPU and the union are acceptable on second reference to this undergraduate political organization founded in 1934. In reference to the constituent parties of the union and their respective members, the following are permissible:

  • On second reference, a reporter may refer to the Party of the Right as the POR. Do not refer to the POR as the right wing or the right.
  • On second reference, a reporter may refer to the Tory Party as the tories.
  • On second reference, a reporter may refer to the Federalist Party as the federalists. Do not use the Feds.
  • On second reference, a reporter may refer to the Conservative Party as the conservatives. Do not abbreviate the party as CP.
  • On second reference, a reporter may refer to the Independent Party as the Independents. Do not abbreviate the party as IP. The Independent Party is technically the original Conservative Party of the YPU. The party decided to change its name decades ago when the majority of its then-members no longer identified as right-leaning. The Conservative Party that exists today formed in 1993 and maintains a bitter feud with the Independents over which of the two factions should rightfully carry on the legacy of the original conservatives.
  • On second reference, a reporter may refer to the Progreessive Party as the progressives. Do not abbreviate the party as PP or refer to its members as the progs.
  • On second reference, a reporter may refer to the Party of the Left as the POL. Do not refer to the POL as the left wing, the left or leftists.
  • Formerly a party of the union, the Socialist Party — once known as the Liberal Party — no longer wields votes for official union events.
