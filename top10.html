Top 10 Copy Rules

  • No Oxford comma!
    • Oranges, lemons and limes. Not oranges, lemons, and limes
  • Class years
    • Handsome Dan ’22. Not Handsome Dan ‘22
    • Check the Yale Directory to verify class years
    • A student in their first year of study at Yale College is a “first year” (with no dash), not a “first-year.” But, the dash is used when the term is an adjective: “First-year students gathered on Cross Campus.”
  • Em dashes ( — )
    • Put spaces on either side of an em dash. The Briton Hadden Memorial Building — located at 202 York St. — is home to the Yale Daily News.
    • On Mac, press option+shift+hyphen to make an em dash. On PC, go to tools → preferences → substitutions, and replace a double hyphen (--) with an em dash (—).
  • Capitalization
    • Titles before are a name are capitalized, and titles after a name are lowercase.
      • Vice President for Global Strategy Pericles Lewis or Pericles Lewis, vice president for global strategy.
      • Except for “professor,” which is always lowercase, unless it is a formal title like Sterling Professor.
    • Majors are lowercase, departments are capitalized
      • The student is a history major. The Department of History recently hired a new professor.
    • Yale University = the University, the Yale Daily News = the News.
  • Numbers
    • All numbers greater than or equal to 10 are written as figures, with commas separating groups of three: 2,435. All numbers less than 10 are spelled out: three, five, nine, etc.
    • There are several exceptions to this rule, including:
      • Spell out numbers at the beginning of sentences, unless the number is a year.
      • Percentages: use numerals and spell out “percent” (3 percent)
      • Ages: She is 8 years old, she is an 8-year-old child.
      • Street numbers in addresses: 5 Hillhouse Ave.
      • Spell out inches, feet, yards, etc: 5 inches of snow, 3 feet wide.
  • Dates
    • Use only numbers, without superscripts: March 1, 2019.
    • When a month is used with a specific date, abbreviate the following months (e.g. Jan. 1; Feb. 29, 2000): Jan., Feb., Aug., Sept., Oct., Nov., Dec.
  • Race-related terminology
    • Capitalize Black, Native and Indigenous.
    • Do not hyphenate dual heritage terms.
      • Asian American, not Asian-American
  • Parentheses
    • Parentheses should never be used to offset tangential thoughts or introduce acronyms. In these cases, an em-dash (—) should be used instead.
    • Parentheses may be used to give statistics for sports teams or identify an actor’s name in a film or theater review.
  • Names and spelling
    • Always double check the spelling of any names you include in your story. This is especially important for a phone or live interview where you may have only heard the name rather than receiving it in an email or reading it online.
  • Academic degrees
    • Try to avoid abbreviations such as B.A. or Ph.D.
    • Instead use Bachelor of Arts, bachelor’s degree, master’s degree, associate degree or doctorate.
  • Active voice
    • Try to use the active voice instead of the passive voice
    • For example, the cat entered the house, not the house was entered by the cat.
